Dr Wangari Maathai

Champion for Earth and Humanity

Wangari Maathai (with shovel), winner of the 2004 Nobel Peace Prize, participates in a tree planting ceremony in the North Garden of UN Headquarters, 2005.

Here's a timeline of Dr. Wangari Maathai's life:

  • 1940 - Born in Nyeri, Kenya
  • 1960 - Wangari Maathai receives a scholarship to study in the United States. She becomes one of the first East African women to earn a degree, studying biology in the United States
  • 1966 - Returns to Kenya and becomes the first woman in East and Central Africa to earn a master's degree in biology from the University of Nairobi.
  • 1971 - Dr. Maathai becomes the Chair of the Department of Veterinary Anatomy at the University of Nairobi, breaking gender barriers in academia.
  • 1977 - Driven by her commitment to environmental conservation and women's rights, Wangari Maathai establishes the Green Belt Movement (GBM). The organization focuses on tree planting, conservation, and empowering women to address environmental and social challenges.
  • 1980 - The Green Belt Movement gains momentum, and its impact is felt nationally and internationally. Dr. Maathai's work becomes a symbol of grassroots activism and environmental stewardship.
  • 1986 - Wangari Maathai earns a Ph.D. in veterinary anatomy from the University of Nairobi, becoming the first African woman to achieve this academic milestone.
  • 2002 - Dr. Maathai is elected as a Member of Parliament in Kenya, representing the Tetu constituency. She uses her political platform to advocate for environmental issues, human rights, and good governance.
  • 2004 - Awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in recognition of her outstanding contribution to sustainable development, democracy, and peace. The Nobel Committee highlights her role in promoting ecologically viable social, economic, and cultural development.
  • 2005 - Dr. Maathai and the Green Belt Movement achieve a historic milestone by planting the billionth tree. This accomplishment reflects their dedication to environmental conservation and the tangible impact of grassroots efforts.
  • 2006 - Publishes her memoir, "Unbowed," providing insights into her life, activism, and the challenges she faced.
  • 2009 - Wangari Maathai passes away on September 25th at the age of 71, succumbing to ovarian cancer. Her death is mourned globally, but her legacy lives on through the Green Belt Movement, her contributions to environmentalism, and her inspiration for future generations of activists.

"We all knew her as a voice of reason, a lady who stood above our artificial divisions of race, tribe and region and championed the cause of humanity."

-- Mwai Kibaki, Former President of Kenya
